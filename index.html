Climate & Emergency Policy

America's Invisible Killer

Heat kills more Americans than hurricanes, tornadoes, and floods combined. The government doesn't classify it as a disaster, and we're not even counting the deaths correctly.

By Ashley Razo  |  April 2026

In late June 2021, Andrew Phelps was directing Oregon's emergency management department when a heat dome pushed Portland to 116°F. Cooling centers were open. Hospitals were not overwhelmed. "We really dodged a bullet," he recalled thinking. Then a reporter called to say the state medical examiner was investigating over 120 deaths. Oregon's official count came in at 123. However, state excess death analysis — comparing that week's mortality to the same week in prior years — found 251 people had died above the expected baseline: twice the official count. No federal disaster declaration was ever issued.

That gap between what is counted and what actually happens is not an unexpected outcome of one bad week. It is how America systematically handles its deadliest weather hazard — one that, across two decades of federal data, kills more people every year than hurricanes, tornadoes, and floods combined.

The Count We Can Prove

The claim that heat deaths are undercounted is widely accepted — but harder to quantify nationally than it might seem. Oregon's 2021 heat dome offers something more solid than expert estimates: two official numbers from government sources, describing the same event.

The difference — 123 versus 251 — reflects deaths where heat worsened an existing condition like heart failure or kidney disease, but never appeared on the certificate. This is not a modeling assumption. It is what Oregon's Health Authority measured directly, by comparing observed deaths to the historical average for that week. The CDC has urged since 2017 that heat be listed as a contributing cause on death certificates even when another condition is listed first. That guidance has not been consistently followed.

A Policy Built for a Different Disaster

The Stafford Act — the federal law empowering FEMA — does not list extreme heat as an eligible disaster. When Hurricane Helene made landfall in 2024, FEMA deployed within hours. When a heat dome settles over a region for two weeks and kills hundreds, no equivalent federal mechanism exists. Cooling centers are funded locally, open only if a city chooses.

"We heard from people who called their uncle Jim at 10 a.m. and Jim was fine — but by 10 p.m. Jim was dead." — Andrew Phelps, Oregon Dept. of Emergency Management, on the 2021 heat dome

As the EPA reports that the average U.S. heat wave season has tripled in length since the 1960s, the mismatch between America's deadliest hazard and its emergency infrastructure is only widening. The 2024 official count of 529 heat deaths is the highest in this dataset. If Oregon's ratio holds, the true figure is closer to 1,000.

America's deadliest disaster doesn't produce satellite imagery or dramatic evacuation footage. It doesn't trigger federal declarations or FEMA convoys. It kills quietly — in apartments without air conditioning, on construction sites, in cars baking in parking lots - and officially, it barely counts.

A note on how disasters are counted: The hurricane average in Fig. 1 excludes 2005, when Katrina alone caused 1,094 deaths. When Katrina is included, hurricanes appear deadlier than heat over this period. That single methodological choice flips the ranking — which itself tells an important story. Hurricane deadliness is concentrated in rare, catastrophic events. Heat kills consistently, year after year, in numbers too small for any single headline but too large to ignore in aggregate. Both patterns are real and neither is well captured by a simple average.
